# Case Study: The $50K Test That Could Have Been $4M

## Traditional Version (Weak)

> "We helped a small business leverage a small testing IDIQ into a long-term customer relationship worth millions over 4 years."

---

## Reframed Version (Urgent)

### The Hidden Problem

A small business won a testing IDIQ. Nothing major — maybe $50K in task orders. The kind of contract that doesn't make anyone's highlight reel.

The PM looked at it and saw exactly what it was on paper: a small job. Low revenue. Not worth his time.

So he sent a field tech to run the tests. Check the boxes. Submit the deliverables. Move on.

### The Escalation

What the PM didn't see:

- Direct access to a government customer
- Face time with the COR and end users
- Insight into upcoming requirements
- A chance to demonstrate capability beyond the scope

Every site visit was an intelligence opportunity. Every deliverable was a trust-building moment. Every conversation was a chance to learn what the agency actually needed next.

But the field tech wasn't trained to see any of that. He ran the tests and left.

### The Near Failure

For 18 months, that company executed the contract flawlessly — and learned nothing.

No follow-on conversations. No relationship with the COR beyond transactional emails. No visibility into the $12M program the testing fed into.

They were invisible to a customer they were already serving.

Meanwhile, a competitor saw the same type of contract differently. They sent someone who understood that every small contract is a door — not a task.

### The Intervention

We trained the ops team on what BD actually looks like in the field:

- How to ask about upcoming requirements without being pushy
- How to document pain points you observe on-site
- How to build relationships with CORs and program managers
- How to feed intel back to BD in real-time

The field tech became a sensor, not just a technician.

### The Stabilization

That competitor? They turned a similar $50K testing contract into a 4-year relationship.

Millions in follow-on work. Sole-source task orders because the customer trusted them. Past performance that opened doors to other agencies.

Same starting point. Same contract type. Different outcome — because one company trained their ops people to see opportunities, and one didn't.

---

## The Loss That Was Prevented (Or Wasn't)

**What most small businesses miss:**

Every ops person on a government site is either:
- Building your next win, or
- Giving it away to a competitor who sees what you don't

The company that lost didn't fail at execution. They failed at recognition.

They had the access. They had the trust. They had 18 months of face time with a customer who was about to spend $12M.

And they sent someone who wasn't trained to see it.
