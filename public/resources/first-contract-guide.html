The No-B.S. Guide to Winning Your First Federal Contract

The government is the biggest customer on earth — it bought $178 billion from small businesses last year. Here's the honest, step-by-step path to becoming one of them. No fluff, no paid "shortcuts," no gatekeeping.

$178.6B to small business (FY2024) $0 cost to register 7 steps
Written by Eric Coffie — GovCon Giants. Built from real federal data, not theory.

Let's be straight with each other. Most "how to win government contracts" advice is either a sales pitch in disguise or a wall of jargon designed to make you feel like you need to pay someone. This isn't that.

Everything in this guide is free to do yourself. The registration is free. The data is public. The path is known. What stops most people isn't difficulty — it's that nobody laid out the steps in plain English. So here they are.

Follow these seven steps in order. Do them one at a time. You do not need all of it done this week — you need to start.

1

FoundationGet your UEI and register in SAM.gov — for free

Before the government can pay you a dollar, you have to exist in their system. That means two things, and both are free:

  • Get a UEI (Unique Entity ID). This replaced the old "DUNS number" in April 2022. You get it directly inside SAM.gov — you don't get it anywhere else.
  • Register in SAM.gov (the System for Award Management). This is your business's official profile with the federal government. It's how agencies find you and how you get paid.

Do this now: Go to SAM.gov, create a Login.gov account, and start your Entity Registration. Budget a few days — the validation step takes time.

⚠ NEVER PAY FOR THIS

SAM.gov registration is 100% free. There are companies that will email or call you offering to "register you for a fee" — some charge hundreds or thousands of dollars for something the government does for free. That's the oldest scam in this business. If someone asks you to pay to register in SAM.gov, walk away.

2

PositioningPick your NAICS codes — what you actually sell

A NAICS code is just a number that describes what your business does. Landscaping has one. IT services has one. Catering has one. The government uses these codes to organize what it buys, and you'll pick the ones that match your business during your SAM.gov registration.

  • Pick the codes that describe what you already do. Don't guess at glamorous ones — pick the ones true to your business.
  • You can have several. Most small businesses list a handful of related codes.
  • These codes become how you'll search for opportunities in the next steps.

3

AdvantageCheck if you qualify for a set-aside

Here's a real advantage most beginners don't know about. The government sets aside a portion of its contracts specifically for certain small businesses — meaning you're only competing against other businesses like yours, not the giant primes.

  • Small business — the baseline. If you're small, you already qualify for set-asides aimed at small business.
  • Woman-Owned (WOSB), Veteran-Owned & Service-Disabled Veteran-Owned (VOSB/SDVOSB), HUBZone, and 8(a) for socially/economically disadvantaged businesses — each opens a lane with less competition.
⚠ Get the veteran path right (it changed)

If you're a veteran: certification for VOSB/SDVOSB moved from the VA (the old "CVE") to the SBA in January 2023. Apply through the SBA now, not the VA's old system. Getting this wrong costs people months.

Do this: figure out which certification(s) fit you. Some are self-certified; others (like 8(a) and HUBZone) require an application. Even the baseline small-business status is worth a lot on its own.

4

The HuntFind opportunities that match you

Now the fun part — seeing what's actually for sale. All of it is public. The catch is that the official sites are clunky and scattered, which is exactly why most people give up here.

  • SAM.gov lists open solicitations — active contracts agencies are trying to award right now.
  • USASpending.gov shows what's already been bought — which tells you who's spending, how much, and who currently holds the work.
  • Search by your NAICS codes from Step 2. Start narrow (your exact service), then widen.

5

The HomeworkStudy ONE target before you chase ten

This is where beginners who win separate from beginners who spin their wheels. Don't spray applications everywhere. Pick one realistic opportunity and study it like it's the only one.

  • Who's buying it? Which agency, which office. Look up what they've bought before on USASpending.
  • Who has it now? Most contracts are re-competed. Find the current holder — that's who you'll have to beat.
  • Read the whole solicitation. The requirements, the deadline, the evaluation criteria. Every word.

This is called capture — doing your homework before the bid opens. One well-researched pursuit beats ten blind applications every time.

6

The RelationshipTalk to a human before the bid drops

Government contracting is not a lottery — it's a relationship business. The single highest-leverage move a beginner can make is reaching out to the right person early, before a contract is even posted.

  • Small Business Liaison Officers (SBLOs) and small-business specialists exist at every agency for exactly one reason: to help small businesses like you. Their contact info is public.
  • A short, respectful email introducing your business and asking about upcoming needs is normal and welcomed. This is how doors open.
  • Do this before the solicitation, not after — once it's posted, they often can't talk to you.
✓ You have permission

Beginners assume they're "not supposed to" contact the government. The opposite is true. These roles were created to help you. Use them.

7

ConsistencyTurn on alerts and show up every week

The people who win aren't smarter — they're consistent. Opportunities that match you appear all the time. The winners see them first and act while everyone else isn't looking.

  • Set up alerts for your NAICS codes so new opportunities come to you instead of you hunting daily.
  • Block one hour a week to review what's new, advance your one target, and send one outreach email.
  • Your first contract may take a few tries. That's normal. Consistency is the whole game.

Do this: automate the search so you never miss a match — then just show up weekly. That's it.
